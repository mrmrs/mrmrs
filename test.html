The Cloudflare Design System

Documentation for a common set of styles and patterns in an attempt to improve design communication across our growing organization. This resource was created for Cloudflare employees, but we hope its helpful for other organizations as well.

The purpose of the screen is to present a visual communication aid between the machine and the user. The elements we place on the screen should all be improving the communication experience for the user. This is a set of tools, documents, assets, and guides that should assist in creating elements that do just that.

Principles

A common shared set of beliefs amongst the Design team as a framework to solve future problems collaboratively.

Empathetic

Design with the user(s) in mind.

Readable

Everything should be easily readable by everyone whether on a large monitor or on a phone in bright sunlight.

Accessible

If you have an internet connection, you should be able to fully utilize Cloudflares tools and services.

High Quality

Our work will reflect the deepest attention to detail. We will use high quality materials.

Performance

Reduce resistance for users wherever possible.

Color

Hierarchy, texture

"In visual perception a color is almost never seen as it really is - as it physically is. This fact makes color the most relative medium in art. In order to use color effectively it is necessary to recognize that color deceives continually."

About

We strive to use color in a way that progressively enhances the visual appeal of our interfaces without relying on a users ability to see color to efficiently. This is a form of progressive enhancement.

We have a set palette of colors weve defined to be used across the product. The majority of the time well want to exclusively use this palette as it enables us to be design consistently implement quickly, and reduce maintenance time.

Over time, this palette may adapt as the look and feel of our interface adapts, but these changes should be deliberate, thoughtful, and thoroughly documented.

Accessible Color Combinations

A curated set of combinations with a contrast ratio above 4.5:1

"People with low vision often have difficulty reading text that does not contrast with its background. This can be exacerbated if the person has a color vision deficiency that lowers the contrast even further. Providing a minimum luminance contrast ratio between the text and its background can make the text more readable even if the person does not see the full range of colors. It also works for the rare individuals who see no color." - Contrast (Enhanced): Understanding SC 1.4.6

Maintaining accessibility is important to us both aesthetically as well as for usability. Harmonious and interchangeable color combinations should be used support the intent of the design. These are colors from our palette that pair with white and a small set of light grays. It's dynamically generated based on the colors in our theme file. Typefaces

Resources and Usage Guidelines

Our primary typeface is Open Sans. This font does not come pre-installed on computers, but can be freely downloaded from Google. Open Sans should be used in headlines and body copy for all distributed marketing materials.

There will be times where Open Sans cannot be used. In cases like these, the use of Helvetica (pre-installed on most Apple computers) and Arial (pre-installed on most every computer) are recommended.

Open Sans

Used across our entire product, print assets, and marketing materials. Open Sans is a sans-serif typeface designed by Steve Matteson and commissioned by Google. According to Google, it was developed with an 'upright stress, open forms and a neutral, yet friendly appearance' and is 'optimized for legibility across print, web, and mobile interfaces.' Featuring wide apertures on many letters and a large x-height (tall lower-case letters), the typeface is highly legible on screen and at small sizes. It belongs to the humanist genre of sans-serif typefaces, with a true italic.

Helvetica Neue

Used in emails we send to customers. A neutral typeface that has great clarity, no intrinsic meaning in its form, and can be used on a wide variety of applications. Its use became a hallmark of the International Typographic Style that emerged from the work of Swiss designers in the 1950s and 60s, becoming one of the most popular typefaces of the 20th century. Over the years, a wide range of variants have been released in different weights, widths and sizes, as well as matching designs for a range of non-Latin alphabets. Notable features of Helvetica as originally designed include the termination of all strokes on horizontal or vertical lines and unusually tight letter spacing, which give it a dense, compact appearance.

Font Weights

Font weights help users understand the hierarchy of information at a glanceable level. Our usage of it should guide the reader's eye to the content they are looking for. For the eye to quickly and accurately find and differentiate different levels and importance of fonts makes text more scannable, readable, and understandable.

Font weights dictate the hierarchy at which content is displayed. Thoughtful combinations and usage will ensure the typography consistent and well-balanced with other UI elements on the page. When in doubt, look through the product and see if there are similar UIs and type-weight usage that would match closesly to what you're building.

Light text (300)

Deemphasized sub headings - should only be used at a font-size of 24px and above

Base text (400)

Used in most cases for headings, descriptions, and labels.

Bold (700)

Used to emphasize words; typically no more than a few. Remember, when everything is bolded, nothing is bolded :)

Measure

The length of the line

While code is often limited to 80 characters per line length, so is body copy. As an experiment pick up any book you can find around you. Chances are each line length is comprised of 9-13 words. Or somewhere between 45-75 characters.

Tracking

Letter-spacing: The space between letters

Numbers or strings of characters and numbers longer than three digits should be tracked to improve readability.

Spacing

A modular scale for consistent visual rhythm

The spacing and proportion of elements are essential to harmony and balance. Our measurements and spacing values are mathematically related to help create a system of interconnected parts that will align with eachother with reduced effort at the implementation stage of design development. The geometrically definable irrational page proportions like 1:1.618 (Golden Section), 1: V^2, 1: V^3, 1: V^5, 1:1.538, and the simple rational proportions of 1:2, 2:3, 5:8 and 5:9 I call clear, intentional and definite. All others are unclear and accidental ratios. The difference between a clear and an unclear ratio, though frequently slight, is noticeable.

Many sites show none of the clear proportions, but accidental ones. We do not know why, but we can demonstrate that a human being finds planes of definite and intentional proportions more pleasant or more beautiful than those of accidental proportions. An ugly format causes an ugly site.

Proper, thoughtful spacing is essential for conveying meaning and establishing visual trust. Spacing is a primary consideration of visual design and using it well and intentionally allows a designer to guide a users eye through a composed layout by elegantly mapping to value, emphasizing grouping(s) and improving legibility. Spacing is both an aesthetic and functional consideration. The ratio of positive:negative space in a composition lays a foundation for the emotive qualities of a design as well as contributes to the amount and quality of information consumable by a user.

Guidelines

In an effort to make interfaces more predictable for users, our icons should be consistent in both usage and aesthetics. Icons should be quickly recognizable when a user is scanning an interface. Icons should be properly aligned with surrouding content so as not to give the user pause. Misaligned icons are a clear sign to the user that obvious details have been ignored by the designers and developers. Icons should be designed in a vector format. Svg should be used where possible. After exporting assets, remove unnecessary metadata in the file. Icons should be designed on a 16x16px grid.

Benefits

There are many benefits to using icons on your user interface. They conserve the space of your controls and make them understandable across cultures. But most of all, icons make your interface actions easier to find. Users wont experience any of these benefits if your icons arent clear and intuitive. These nine guidelines are important in knowing how to design and display your icons. Let them guide you, but dont let them confine you.
